## Writing Stance

- Short, direct, confident.
- Expert but not noisy.
- Outcome-first, not process-first.
- Specific to startup founders, not generic B2B buyers.

## Rules

1. Lead with the founder outcome, not internal process.
2. Keep hero copy tight: headline 4–8 words, subcopy 1–2 short sentences.
3. Every section headline must communicate business value.
4. Use active verbs: build, launch, scale, convert, automate, ship.
5. Replace vague words with concrete value language.
6. Avoid buzzword stacking and generic claims.
7. Reinforce this narrative: Yuvabe helps founders build the right thing, then iterate toward traction across product, engineering, and growth marketing.
8. Fit copy to the component and layout system; do not write oversized paragraphs that fight the design.

## Website Section Patterns

- **Hero**: one sharp promise, one practical supporting sentence, one main CTA.
- **Capabilities**: describe outcomes and leverage, not service menus.
- **Process**: explain how Yuvabe helps founders decide, build, and learn.
- **Proof**: turn credibility into concrete signals, not vague praise.
- **CTA blocks**: reduce friction and make the next step obvious.

## About Page Principles

- Personal, founder-close voice: conversational, grounded, human, and calm.
- Lead with who we are and how we work, then connect to why founders trust us.
- Mention Auroville as real context, not decorative brand language.
- Keep AI-first framing practical and light; mention early, do not repeat across every section.
- Values should sound lived-in and behavioral, not like manifesto lines.

## CTA Guidelines

- Primary: `Start Your Project` / `Talk Through Your Roadmap` / `View Our Work`
- Secondary: `How We Work` / `See Case Studies` / `See More Work`
- Avoid vague meeting CTAs like `Book a Founder Call`; prefer next-step CTAs that say what happens.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing copy, verify:
1. Can a founder understand the value in under 5 seconds?
2. Is the message specific, concrete, and relevant to startups?
3. Is AI-first positioning explicit and credible?
4. Are CTAs clear, action-led, and distinct?
5. Is the copy short, focused, and appropriate for the component?
6. Does it sound like a real person?
7. Does the copy pass the Copy Swap Test — could a competitor use the same words?
8. Do headline and key lines still read well when they wrap in the actual layout?

## The Copy Swap Test

Remove all logos and visuals. Read the copy out loud. Ask: "Could someone else use the same words on their site?" If yes, rewrite — it is too vague.

## 3 Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Too long**: Short copy is hard. Rewriting a few lines many times is normal.
2. **Too generic**: Generic copy leaves readers uninterested. Make every claim specific.
3. **Unnecessary & duplicated**: Extra sentences and repeated information add noise.

## Design-System-Aware Copy Rules

- Write for scannable components and sections, not long-form page blobs.
- Keep labels, helper text, card headings, and CTA text concise enough to work inside reusable UI patterns.
- Use one dominant CTA intent per section so the system does not create competing actions.
- Write headlines so they wrap cleanly across likely desktop and mobile widths.
